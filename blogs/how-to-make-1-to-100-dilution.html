How to Mix a 1:100 Dilution (Exact Amounts for Bottles + Buckets)

1:100 is common on concentrate labels—and easy to misread. This gives you exact amounts for real containers without over-strong mixing.

10–14 min read • Ratio basics

Part of the main guide

This article belongs to the Cleaning Dilution Guide, where readers can move between ratio meaning, exact bottle math, bucket scaling, and common real-world dilution examples.

Quick answer

A 1:100 dilution means 1 part concentrate + 100 parts water. In practical terms, the concentrate is about ~1% of the water amount (slightly under 1% of the final mix). If you want the safest no-math route, use the Cleaning Dilution Calculator and enter 1:100 with your container size.

Exact amounts for common container sizes

These are practical, easy-to-measure amounts that get you very close to a true 1:100 label ratio. If your label gives a different method (like “mL per liter” or “oz per gallon”), follow the label and scale it.

  • 500 mL bottle: about 5 mL concentrate + water to fill
  • 1 liter bottle: about 10 mL concentrate + water to fill
  • 16 oz bottle: about 0.16 oz concentrate (≈ 1 tsp) + water to fill
  • 32 oz bottle: about 0.32 oz concentrate (≈ 2 tsp) + water to fill
  • 3 L bucket: about 30 mL concentrate + water to fill
  • 5 L bucket: about 50 mL concentrate + water to fill
  • 10 L bucket: about 100 mL concentrate + water to fill

If you prefer ratio guides nearby, these help anchor your intuition: What Does 1:50 Mean? and How to Calculate Dilution Ratio.

The two label traps that cause wrong mixes

  • Trap #1: Parts vs total. Some people read 1:100 as “1 part in 100 total,” but most cleaning labels mean 1 part concentrate to 100 parts water. When in doubt, don’t guess—use the calculator.
  • Trap #2: Measuring “to the line” without accounting for concentrate. If you pour concentrate first and then fill water to the top, you can drift off-ratio depending on the bottle markings. It’s usually more repeatable to add most of the water first, then measure concentrate, then top up.

A repeatable mixing method (no guesswork)

  1. Check the label first. Confirm it actually calls for 1:100 for your task and surface.
  2. Pick your container (500 mL, 1 L, 32 oz, bucket).
  3. Use the Cleaning Dilution Calculator to enter 1:100 and your container size to get exact mL/oz.
  4. Add water first (about 80–90% of the container).
  5. Measure and add concentrate, then top up with water to final volume.
  6. Label it (product name, ratio, date) if you’re storing it.

If your label says “mL per liter” instead of ratios, use: How to Scale a Label That Says mL per Liter.

When 1:100 is a good fit (and when it isn’t)

1:100 is often used for light-to-moderate everyday cleaning where you’re maintaining surfaces rather than stripping heavy soil. But the correct ratio still depends on the specific product, the surface, and the job. If the label gives different ratios for floors vs bathrooms vs degreasing, follow those.

If you’re cleaning food-contact surfaces, don’t assume a concentrate is safe for that use—follow the label instructions for approved surfaces and any required rinse steps.

Common 1:100 mistakes

  • Over-strong mixing: can leave residue, cause streaks, or irritate skin—especially with fragranced concentrates.
  • Under-mixing: can reduce performance and lead to “double cleaning.”
  • Inconsistent measuring: “caps” and “splashes” aren’t repeatable. Use a teaspoon, measuring cup, or syringe.

FAQ

Is 1:100 the same as 1%?

It’s close, but not identical depending on whether you count “parts” as water-only or total volume. For practical cleaning mixes, it’s usually close enough—what matters more is following your product label and measuring consistently.

How do I mix 1:100 for a spray bottle without doing math?

Use the Cleaning Dilution Calculator, enter 1:100, then your bottle size. Measure the concentrate, add water, and you’re done.

My label says “10 mL per liter.” Is that basically 1:100?

Often it’s in the same neighborhood, but treat “mL per liter” as the authoritative instruction and scale it exactly: How to scale mL per liter labels.
