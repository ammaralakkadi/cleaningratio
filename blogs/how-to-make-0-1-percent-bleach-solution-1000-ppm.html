How to Make a 0.1% Bleach Solution (1000 ppm): Label-First Mixing for Common Bottle Sizes

0.1% is a common “target strength” people search for. The only safe way to mix it is to start with the bleach % on your label and measure from there.

12 min read • Bleach dilution

Part of the main guide

This article belongs to the Bleach Dilution Guide, where readers can move between bleach ppm targets, dilution basics, safety warnings, and practical bleach-use articles.

Quick answer

A 0.1% bleach solution is roughly 1000 ppm available chlorine. To make it correctly, you must first check your bleach label for the starting concentration (often 5–8.25%). Then use the Bleach Dilution Calculator to get an exact measured amount for your bottle or bucket. If your product label gives different dilution directions for your intended use, follow the product label instructions.

Step 1: find the bleach % on the label (this changes everything)

“Household bleach” is not one fixed strength. The front brand name doesn’t matter here — the percentage on the label does. If you mix a 0.1% target using the wrong assumed %, you can end up too weak (ineffective) or too strong (more fumes, more surface risk).

If you’re new to this, also read: Bleach Dilution Mistakes to Avoid. Most problems happen before the bleach even touches the surface.

What “0.1%” means in practice

“0.1%” is a concentration target in the final mixed solution. You’re taking a stronger bleach and diluting it down. The higher your starting bleach %, the smaller the amount you’ll measure into the water.

If you like the quick mental model: for a 5% bleach, 0.1% is roughly a 1:49 style dilution (very close to 1 part bleach + 49 parts water). For stronger bleaches, the ratio gets even more "water-heavy." But don't eyeball it — measure it with the PPM Dilution Calculator.

The clean way: use the calculator and measure once

Use: Bleach Dilution Calculator. Enter your bleach % from the label, pick your target (0.1%), and enter your container volume. You’ll get an exact amount to measure.

If you’re making a spray bottle mix specifically, this pairs well with: How to Dilute Bleach for a Spray Bottle.

Practical examples (common bottle sizes)

The numbers below are provided as practical examples for common label strengths. If your bleach label differs, don’t “force match” — use the calculator. And again: follow the product label instructions for the specific use-case.

Measuring tip: For small amounts, use a measuring spoon or syringe-style kitchen/measuring tool you dedicate to cleaning. “A splash” is how people accidentally double the strength.

0.1% from 5% bleach

  • 500 mL bottle: 10 mL bleach + 490 mL water
  • 1 L bottle: 20 mL bleach + 980 mL water
  • 32 oz bottle: ~19 mL bleach + water to 32 oz
  • 5-gallon bucket: ~378 mL bleach + water to 5 gallons

0.1% from 8.25% bleach

  • 500 mL bottle: ~6 mL bleach + water to 500 mL
  • 1 L bottle: ~12 mL bleach + water to 1 L
  • 32 oz bottle: ~11 mL bleach + water to 32 oz
  • 5-gallon bucket: ~229 mL bleach + water to 5 gallons

These are intentionally “measure-first” examples. If you want exactness for your specific bottle size, use the Bleach Dilution Calculator.

Safety notes that actually prevent problems

  • Never mix bleach with other cleaners. Especially acids (like vinegar) or ammonia-based products.
  • Ventilation matters. If you smell strong fumes, stop and increase airflow.
  • Use cool water. Hot water can increase odor and breakdown.
  • Keep it off fabrics and metals unless the product label allows it. Bleach can discolor and corrode.
  • Follow the product label instructions for contact time, surfaces, and where it should not be used.

How long it lasts (and why “make it fresh” is usually correct)

Diluted bleach doesn’t stay at full strength forever. Light, heat, and time all reduce potency. If you’re mixing for a purpose where strength matters, you should understand storage and remake timing: How Long Does Diluted Bleach Last? Storage, Potency, and When to Remix.
