How to Scale “oz per gallon” Cleaning Labels to Any Spray Bottle (16 oz, 32 oz, 1 L)

“oz per gallon” is simple once you treat it as a fixed dose per 128 fl oz. The only goal is scaling cleanly to your bottle size—without rounding mistakes.

12–15 min read • Label scaling

Part of the main guide

This article belongs to the Cleaning Dilution Guide, where readers can move between gallon-based label scaling, bottle recipes, ratio basics, and label-reading help.

Quick answer

If a label says X oz per gallon, it means you use X ounces of concentrate for 1 gallon (128 fl oz) of water unless the label explicitly says “make 1 gallon total.” To scale it to any bottle: concentrate (oz) = bottle size (fl oz) × (X ÷ 128). Follow the product label instructions exactly, especially when labels distinguish “per gallon of water” vs “makes one gallon total.”

If you want a fast, repeatable method (especially when your container is metric), use the Cleaning Dilution Calculator and save your recipe for next time.

What “oz per gallon” really means (and the label trap)

Most concentrates are written as a dosing rule: “2 oz per gallon” or “4 oz per gallon.” That is usually shorthand for “per gallon of water,” not “make one gallon total.” Some products do mean “make 1 gallon total solution,” and the math changes slightly.

  • If the label says “X oz per gallon of water”, you scale against 128 fl oz of water.
  • If the label says “to make 1 gallon”, the “water amount” is slightly less because concentrate is included in the final gallon.
  • If your label gives both (common in commercial products), follow the exact line for your use-case.

Keep this open if labels confuse you: How to Read Cleaning Dilution Instructions on Labels.

The scaling formula (one line, no guessing)

Let’s say your label says X oz per gallon. A gallon is 128 fl oz. Your bottle is B fl oz.

  • Concentrate (oz) = B × (X ÷ 128)
  • Concentrate (mL) = Concentrate (oz) × 29.57 (useful if you measure in mL)

If you’d rather not convert units manually, use the Cleaning Dilution Calculator (especially for 500 mL / 750 mL / 1 L bottles).

Quick table: common bottles (for a label that says 1 oz per gallon)

This table is for 1 oz per gallon as a clean baseline. If your label is 2 oz per gallon, double the concentrate amounts. If it’s 4 oz per gallon, multiply by four.

Container size Concentrate for 1 oz/gal Notes
16 fl oz spray bottle 0.125 oz (≈ 3.7 mL) Easy to over-pour. Measure if you can.
24 fl oz bottle 0.188 oz (≈ 5.6 mL) Good “mid-size” for daily cleaners.
32 fl oz spray bottle 0.25 oz (≈ 7.4 mL) Very common label conversion target.
500 mL bottle ≈ 4.0 mL Metric container; calculator is faster.
1 liter bottle ≈ 8.0 mL Also scales cleanly for repeated mixes.

Step-by-step (the repeatable way)

  1. Read the label line carefully and confirm whether it means per gallon of water or makes one gallon total.
  2. Pick your container size (16 oz, 32 oz, 500 mL, 1 L).
  3. Use the formula (or the Cleaning Dilution Calculator) to get the concentrate amount.
  4. Measure concentrate with a consistent tool (measuring spoon, syringe-style measure, or marked cup). “A random cap” is not consistent.
  5. Add water using the label’s method (some are water-first; some are “fill to line”).
  6. Write the recipe on tape and stick it to the bottle. Consistency beats “stronger.”

Common mistakes that make mixes too strong

  • Treating “oz per gallon” like a ratio (it’s dosing, and rounding errors add up fast in small bottles).
  • Forgetting a gallon is 128 fl oz (not 100, not “about 120”).
  • Guessing with caps or “free-pouring,” then wondering why residue/streaks happen.
  • Mixing stronger “because it’s dirty.” Over-strong mixes can leave film and may damage surfaces.
