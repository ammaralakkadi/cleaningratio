Simple Green Dilution Ratio Chart (All-Purpose, Floors, Degreasing): Use Your Label + Bottle Math

Simple Green dilution questions are usually math questions. This turns your label into exact amounts for your spray bottle or bucket—cleanly and repeatably.

14–18 min read • Spray bottles + buckets

Quick answer

Dilute Simple Green using the exact instruction printed on your specific product (concentrate vs ready-to-use). If your label uses a ratio (like 1:10 or 1:30), plug the ratio and your container size into the Cleaning Dilution Calculator, measure the concentrate amount, then top up with water to the final volume.

If your label is written as “oz per gallon” or “mL per liter,” keep it in that format and scale it instead of guessing. Start here: How to Read Cleaning Dilution Instructions on Labels.

The 4 mistakes that cause “it didn’t work” (or “it left residue”)

  • Using the wrong product type. RTU is not meant to be diluted, and concentrate is not meant to be used straight unless the label says so.
  • Mixing to the wrong final volume. People pour water first, foam, then underfill/overfill the bottle.
  • Eyeballing small amounts. For weaker dilutions, small errors can double the concentrate.
  • Using “stronger” as a shortcut. Too-strong mixes can leave film, require more rinsing, or be harsh on finishes.

Step 1: Read the Simple Green label the way it’s written

“Simple Green dilution ratio” searches usually assume one universal chart. In reality, labels vary by product version and region. The safest approach is label-first: copy the dilution instruction exactly, then convert it to your container.

Ratio (parts) 1:10, 1:30, 1:64 (examples) Use the Cleaning Dilution Calculator for your final bottle/bucket volume.
Ounces per gallon “X oz per 1 gal” Scale proportionally. Related: How much concentrate for 1 gallon of water?

Step 2: Convert your dilution to the container you use

Most people mix Simple Green in one of two ways: a spray bottle for daily cleaning, or a bucket/mop bucket for floors and large surfaces. The conversion method is the same—only the final volume changes.

Spray bottles: the most common sizes (and what to do)

  • 32 oz (very common): use the calculator for your label ratio, measure concentrate, top up to 32 oz. If you want a focused guide: 32 oz spray bottle dilution.
  • 16 oz / 24 oz: same process, smaller volume—don’t reuse 32 oz numbers. Helpful: 16 oz spray bottle dilution.
  • 500 mL / 750 mL / 1 L: use the same ratio conversion, but in metric container sizes.

For “how much concentrate” searches (without brand), these are tightly related: How much concentrate for a 500 mL spray bottle? and How much concentrate for 1 liter?

Buckets and mop buckets (3 L, 5 L, 10 L)

Buckets are where people accidentally “free pour” and end up with inconsistent mixes week to week. Pick a bucket size you actually fill to (or mark a fill line once), then mix to that final volume.

A practical walkthrough is here: Cleaning Dilution for a Mop Bucket (3L, 5L, 10L Examples).

Measuring without making it complicated

Precision doesn’t mean fancy tools. It means using the same tool every time and not guessing.

  • For small concentrate amounts: use mL markings, a small graduated cup, or a syringe-style kitchen measure.
  • For medium amounts: a measuring cup works fine.
  • Avoid “capfuls” unless you measure the cap once and write down its volume.
  • Add concentrate first, then water (less foam = more accurate final volume).

Residue, rinsing, and why dilution matters on floors

Floors and large surfaces are where over-concentration becomes obvious: film, streaking, or a “sticky” feel after drying. If that happens, the first fix is not a new product—it’s returning to the label dilution and rinsing guidance.

  • If the label says rinse, rinse. This is common for surfaces that will be touched often or may contact food.
  • Use the lowest effective dilution. Stronger mixes can increase residue and cleaning effort.
  • Spot test on finished wood, delicate coatings, and painted surfaces.

Don’t mix cleaners (especially for “boosting”)

Mixing products to “boost power” is where people accidentally create irritating fumes or reduce performance. Unless a label explicitly says it’s compatible, treat combinations as “don’t.”

If you want the short safety versions your site already has: bleach + ammonia, bleach + vinegar, bleach + dish soap.

FAQ

Is there one universal Simple Green dilution chart?

Not reliably. Labels can vary by product version and region. The safest approach is to use your bottle’s instruction, then convert it to your container size using the calculator or direct scaling.

What’s the fastest way to get exact amounts for a 32 oz bottle?

Use the Cleaning Dilution Calculator with your label ratio and “32 oz.” Measure the concentrate amount it returns, then top up with water to 32 oz.

If I’m cleaning something greasy, can I just make it stronger?

Stronger isn’t always better. It can leave residue and may not be intended for that surface. First, match the label’s intended use and dilution, give it time to work, and use agitation (brush/cloth) instead of overconcentrating.

Can I pre-mix and store it?

If your label allows storage guidance, follow it. Practically: label the bottle with dilution + date mixed, store it sealed, and mix smaller batches if you notice performance changes over time.
