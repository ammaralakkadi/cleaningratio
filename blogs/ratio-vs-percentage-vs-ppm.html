Ratio vs Percentage vs PPM — What's the Difference?

Three ways of expressing the same thing: how much concentrate is in the final solution. Here's how they relate and when you'll see each one.

7 min read • Concept guide

Part of the main guide

This concept guide bridges the Cleaning Dilution Guide and the Bleach Dilution Guide — because bleach content uses all three units (ppm charts, ratio instructions, percentage labels) and every calculator on the site works in at least one of them.

Quick answer

Ratio, percentage, and ppm are three ways of describing concentration — how much active ingredient ends up in your final solution. A 1:10 dilution, roughly 9.1%, and roughly 91,000 ppm all describe the same strength. The numbers look wildly different, but they're just different scales for the same measurement.

You don't need to memorize conversions. The site's calculators handle the math. But understanding which unit you're looking at helps you read labels correctly and pick the right calculator.

What a ratio means

A ratio like 1:10 means 1 part concentrate to 10 parts water — giving you 11 total parts. This is the format you'll see most often on cleaning product labels and janitorial instructions.

Common ratios on labels: 1:10, 1:32, 1:64, 1:128, 1:256. The bigger the second number, the weaker the solution. A 1:128 mix is far more diluted than a 1:10 mix.

For a detailed breakdown of how ratios work, see How to Calculate Dilution Ratio or start with a specific example like What Does 1:10 Dilution Mean? or What Does 1:32 Dilution Mean?.

What a percentage means

Percentage describes the concentration of active ingredient in the total solution. A 1% solution means 1 part active ingredient per 100 total parts.

You'll see percentages on product bottles: bleach at 3%, 5%, or 8.25% sodium hypochlorite; hydrogen peroxide at 3%, 12%, or 35%. These tell you the starting concentration before dilution — which matters because your final diluted strength depends on what you started with.

For the math behind converting a ratio to a percentage, see Dilution Ratio to Percentage Conversion.

What ppm means

PPM stands for parts per million. It's the same idea as percentage, just on a much finer scale. 1% = 10,000 ppm. So 0.1% = 1,000 ppm, and 0.01% = 100 ppm.

PPM shows up when small concentrations matter — which is exactly the case for disinfection. Common targets:

  • 200 ppm — food-contact surface sanitizing
  • 500 ppm — general surface disinfection
  • 1,000 ppm — heavy disinfection (illness, bodily fluids)
  • 5,000 ppm — mold remediation, biohazard cleanup

For a complete reference, see the Bleach PPM Chart (500 / 1,000 / 5,000).

Conversion quick-reference

Ratio Percentage PPM Typical use
1:10 ~9.1% ~91,000 Heavy-duty / mold cleanup
1:50 ~2% ~20,000 Strong cleaning
1:100 ~1% ~10,000 General disinfection
1:1,000 ~0.1% ~1,000 Standard surface disinfection
1:10,000 ~0.01% ~100 Light sanitizing

These are approximations. Exact values depend on whether the label means "1 part concentrate + 10 parts water" (1:10 = 11 total) or "1 part in 10 total." Most cleaning labels mean the first — 1 part added to the second number.

When you'll see each unit

Ratios appear on consumer cleaning product labels, janitorial supply instructions, and concentrate packaging. If a label says "dilute 1:64," it's speaking in ratios.

Percentages appear on bottles themselves — the sodium hypochlorite percentage on bleach, the acetic acid percentage on vinegar, the concentration on hydrogen peroxide. They describe what's in the bottle before you dilute it.

PPM appears in food safety regulations, health department requirements, water treatment specs, institutional cleaning protocols, and test strip readings. If someone asks you to hit "500 ppm free chlorine," they're speaking in ppm.

FAQ

How do I convert ppm to percent?

Divide by 10,000. So 1,000 ppm = 0.1%, and 5,000 ppm = 0.5%. Going the other way, multiply percent by 10,000. The Dilution Ratio to Percentage Conversion article walks through the full math.

Is 1:10 the same as 10%?

Close, but not exactly. 1:10 means 1 part concentrate to 10 parts water = 11 total parts. That's actually about 9.1%. The difference is small at lower ratios but grows at higher concentrations. For practical cleaning purposes, the approximation is usually close enough — but if precision matters (like hitting a specific ppm target), use a calculator.

Why do some labels use ratios and others use ppm?

Different audiences. Consumer cleaning labels use ratios because they're the easiest to follow with a measuring cup. Institutional and regulatory contexts use ppm because they need exact concentrations for compliance. Percentages sit in between — they describe what's in the bottle so you can calculate either one.

Which unit is most accurate?

PPM is the most precise because it operates on the finest scale. But accuracy depends on measurement, not units. A carefully measured 1:100 ratio is more accurate than a sloppy ppm guess. Use whichever unit your label or protocol specifies, and let the calculator handle the conversion.
