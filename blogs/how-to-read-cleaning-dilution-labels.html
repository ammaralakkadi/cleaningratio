How to Read Cleaning Dilution Instructions on Labels

Labels describe dilution in a few common formats. Once you know what you’re looking at, converting it into exact mL or liters is simple (and fast with a calculator).

6 min read • Label decoding

Quick answer

Most cleaning labels give dilution as either a ratio (like 1:10 or 1:50), an amount per liter (like 20 mL per 1 L), or sometimes a percentage. Once you identify the format, you can convert it to your container size using the Cleaning Dilution Calculator.

Format 1: Ratios (1:10, 1:20, 1:50)

Ratio labels usually describe parts of product compared to parts of water. A common example is 1:10. In many cleaning contexts, that means 1 part product + 10 parts water.

If you want the plain explanation with examples, read: What Does 1:10 Dilution Mean?

Quick example (1:50, 1 liter total):

  • Total parts = 1 + 50 = 51
  • Product = 1000 mL ÷ 51 = ~19.6 mL
  • Water = the rest to reach 1 liter

If you don’t want to do the math, use the Cleaning Dilution Calculator.

Format 2: “mL per liter” (example: 20 mL per 1 L)

This is the easiest format. You scale it up or down depending on your container size.

Label says Your container Product amount
20 mL per 1 liter 500 mL bottle 10 mL
20 mL per 1 liter 3 liter bucket 60 mL
20 mL per 1 liter 5 liter bucket 100 mL

Mop bucket examples here: Cleaning Dilution for a Mop Bucket

Format 3: Percentages (%)

Some labels use percentages, but they’re less common in everyday household instructions. If your label uses percent, follow the label wording carefully and prefer the manufacturer’s exact method. When in doubt, choose a product label format you can measure confidently (ratio or mL/L).

If you’re converting to exact amounts for a bottle or bucket, the Cleaning Dilution Calculator is the safest shortcut.

A common question: “Total mix” or “add X to water”?

Labels usually mean one of these:

  • Add product to a set amount of water (example: “20 mL per 1 L of water”).
  • Make a final total volume (example: “make 1 liter of solution”).

If the label isn’t clear, don’t guess—use the label’s example sizes (if provided) or follow the manufacturer’s directions.

The fastest reliable method

  1. Read the label and identify the format (ratio / mL per liter / percent).
  2. Decide your container size (500 mL, 1 L, 5 L, etc.).
  3. Use the Cleaning Dilution Calculator to get exact amounts.
  4. Measure, mix, and repeat the same method next time for consistency.

For bleach-specific label ratios, use: Bleach Dilution Calculator and avoid mixing bleach with other cleaners.

Common label-reading mistakes

  • Confusing ratio meaning (especially 1:10 and 1:50)
  • Mixing liters and mL (bucket mixing is where it happens most)
  • Using one dilution for every task (labels often vary by use)
  • Eyeballing amounts instead of measuring once
