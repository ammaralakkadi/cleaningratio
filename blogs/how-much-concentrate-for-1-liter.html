How Much Cleaner Concentrate for 1 Liter of Solution?

Quick 1-liter mixing examples for common dilution ratios—so you can measure accurately and repeat results.

5 min read • 1 liter mixing

Quick answer

The amount of concentrate depends on the dilution on your product label (for example 1:10, 1:20, 1:50, or “mL per liter”). For a fast, accurate result, use the Cleaning Dilution Calculator and enter 1,000 mL as your final volume.

Quick 1 liter examples for common ratios

These are rounded to practical measuring amounts. If your label uses a different ratio, use the calculator for an exact match.

Label ratio Concentrate (approx.) Water to fill to 1 liter
1:10 (1 + 10 parts) ~91 mL ~909 mL
1:20 (1 + 20 parts) ~48 mL ~952 mL
1:50 (1 + 50 parts) ~20 mL ~980 mL
1:100 (1 + 100 parts) ~10 mL ~990 mL

Need the meaning behind ratios like 1:10? Read: What Does 1:10 Dilution Mean?

If your label says “mL per liter”

This is the simplest format for 1-liter mixing. If the label says “X mL per 1 liter,” you use that exact amount for a 1-liter mix.

Example: Label says “20 mL per 1 liter”

  • Measure 20 mL concentrate
  • Add water to reach 1 liter total

Simple mixing steps (clean and consistent)

  1. Check the label for the correct dilution for your task.
  2. Add most of the water to your container first.
  3. Measure the concentrate amount and add it.
  4. Top up with water to reach 1 liter total, then gently swirl.

Common mistakes with 1 liter mixes

  • Using “a capful” without knowing the volume: measure once so you can repeat it.
  • Mixing stronger than the label: can leave residue or damage finishes.
  • Using the wrong ratio for the job: some labels give different ratios for light vs heavy cleaning.
  • Confusing ratio meaning: most labels mean 1 part product + X parts water.

When the calculator is the best option

If you’re switching ratios often or using unusual sizes (like 650 mL or 3 liters), the Cleaning Dilution Calculator is the fastest way to avoid mistakes.

Safety note (short)

Follow product label instructions and surface care guidance. If you’re using bleach products, don’t mix bleach with other cleaners (especially ammonia, acids, or vinegar).
