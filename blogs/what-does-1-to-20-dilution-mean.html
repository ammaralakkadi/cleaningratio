What Does a 1:20 Dilution Mean?

A calm, calculator-backed explanation of 1:20 (what it actually means), plus exact amounts for common bottles and buckets so you don’t guess.

10–12 min read • Updated guide

Part of the main guide

This article belongs to the Cleaning Dilution Guide, where readers can move between ratio basics, bottle calculations, bucket examples, and label-reading help.

Quick answer

A 1:20 dilution typically means 1 part concentrate mixed with 20 parts water. That creates 21 total parts in the finished solution.

If you want exact measurements (mL, oz, tablespoons) for your bottle or bucket, use the Cleaning Dilution Calculator and match the ratio shown on your product label.

Safety note: Always follow the product label instructions first (some labels use “1:20” differently), and never mix products unless the label explicitly allows it.

What “1:20” means (and the one mistake that causes most mis-mixes)

The most common mistake is treating “1:20” as if it means “1 part concentrate in 20 total parts.” In most cleaning labels, it’s 1 part concentrate + 20 parts water (so the final mix is 21 parts total).

If your label instead says something like “dilute to 1:20” or “1:20 final”, the intention may be different. When in doubt, rely on the label’s dosing examples (mL per liter, oz per gallon) or use your label’s support line.

If you want a deeper walkthrough of label wording, see How to Read Cleaning Dilution Instructions on Labels.

The simple formula (so you can scale any container)

For a label-style 1:20 mix (1 part concentrate + 20 parts water), the concentrate amount is:

  • Concentrate = Final Volume ÷ 21
  • Water = Final Volume − Concentrate

If you’d rather not do math (and avoid rounding mistakes), plug the ratio into the Cleaning Dilution Calculator and choose your final volume.

For more ratio basics (parts vs total), this pairs well with How to Calculate Dilution Ratio.

Exact amounts for common bottles (1:20 label-style)

These examples assume the common meaning: 1 part concentrate + 20 parts water. Measure accurately, especially for stronger concentrates.

1 L (1000 mL) ≈ 48 mL ≈ 952 mL
32 oz (1 quart) ≈ 1.5 oz ≈ 30.5 oz

Bucket examples (3 L, 5 L, 10 L)

Bucket mixing is where people most often “free-pour.” If your label calls for 1:20, measure once, then keep the recipe.

Bucket Size Concentrate Water
3 L ≈ 143 mL ≈ 2.86 L
5 L ≈ 238 mL ≈ 4.76 L
10 L ≈ 476 mL ≈ 9.52 L

If you mop often, this connects well with Cleaning Dilution for a Mop Bucket (3L, 5L, 10L Examples).

How to mix 1:20 reliably (without making a mess)

  • Start with the label. Confirm it truly means 1 part concentrate to 20 parts water.
  • Add water first (especially in small bottles) to reduce foaming and splashing.
  • Measure the concentrate with a marked cap, syringe, or measuring cup you keep for cleaning use.
  • Top up with water to the final volume and gently mix.
  • Label the bottle (product name + ratio + date) so you don’t “double-dose” later.

Common 1:20 label traps to watch

  • Parts vs total: most labels mean “1 + 20,” not “1 in 20 total.”
  • Different units: some labels give dosing like “mL per liter” or “oz per gallon.” If you see that, use it—it’s clearer than ratios.
  • Hard water adjustments: some concentrates require different dosing in hard water—follow the label.
  • Mixing with other products: don’t combine cleaners unless the label specifically says it’s safe.
